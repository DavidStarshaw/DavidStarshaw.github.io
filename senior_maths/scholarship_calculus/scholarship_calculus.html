Introduction

Scholarship calculus is a demanding course designed to test students to their limits1. Students will need to be able to use skills from any of the level 3 calculus standards including standards that aren't covered in the Wā Ora level 3 calculus course. The tasks set often require skills from more than one area, requiring students to combine different skills in ways they haven't seen before. This course sets up the students with the advanced skills they need but the tasks will always demand thinking on your feet and inventing creative approaches on the spot.

So far I haven't made any resources for scholarship calculus. But there are a lot of resources I've pulled into one place for your convenience.

Curriculum links

The questions in this course can come from any of the level 8 New Zealand Curriculum calculus achievement objectives. Specifically:

Patterns and relationships

  • M 8-1 Apply the geometry of conic sections (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
  • M 8-2 Display and interpret the graphs of functions with the graphs of their inverse and/or reciprocal functions (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
  • M 8-3 Use permutations and combinations (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
  • M 8-4 Use curve fitting, log modelling, and linear programming techniques (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
  • M 8-5 Develop network diagrams to find optimal solutions, including critical paths (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
Equations and expressions
  • M 8-6 Manipulate trigonometric expressions
  • M 8-7 Form and use trigonometric, polynomial, and other non-linear equations
  • M 8-8 Form and use systems of simultaneous equations, including three linear equations and three variables, and interpret the solutions in context
  • M 8-9 Manipulate complex numbers and present them graphically (not normally covered in level 3 calculus)
Calculus
  • M 8-10 Identify discontinuities and limits of functions
  • M 8-11 Choose and apply a variety of differentiation, integration, and anti-differentiation techniques to functions and relations, using both analytical and numerical methods
  • M 8-12 Form differential equations and interpret the solutions
