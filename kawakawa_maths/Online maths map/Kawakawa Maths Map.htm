27L5

The statistical enquiry cycle

Determine appropriate variables and how to measure them; consider sources of variation; consider data collection methods, and conduct investigations using the statistical enquiry cycle: gather, sort, clean and display data of different kinds and of many variables in multiple ways to answer questions; find patterns, variations, relationships and trends in data; compare sample distributions visually, using measures of centre, spread, and proportion; and communicate findings in a report.

 

The statistical enquiry cycle (PPDAC):

 

Sample inquiry questions:

Summary questions: What is the normal height of a 14-year-old female?

Comparative questions: Do males do more exercise than females?

Relational questions: example, is there a relationship between television watching and lack of exercise?

 

To consider when planning data collection:

Representative sampling; adequate sample size; avoidance of bias in surveys and sampling techniques; and systematic collection and processing of data that does not narrow potential responses; and technology use.

 

Expected ways of displaying data at Level 5:

Tally charts, frequency tables, pictographs, bar graphs, strip graphs, and pie charts for category data; dot plots, stem and leaf graphs and scatterplots for measurement data; and line graphs for time series data; and measures of centrality and spread such as mean or median, range and quartiles - this means that displays such as box and whisker plots and histograms are used.

 

Data analysis:

Data should be analysed by comparing distributions visually using multiple graph types, preferably generated by technology to  look for differences between distributions, for example, the median of one group is higher than the upper quartile of the other; it should be recognised that all findings from the analysis of samples must be interpreted with uncertainty and caution regarding generalising the results to the whole of the sampled population.

31L3

Probability

Know that probability is about the chance of outcomes occurring, and that it is not possible to know the exact probability of something occurring in most everyday situations; use  listing, tree diagrams with equally likely outcomes, or tables to find all the possible outcomes of simple situations such as tossing coins, drawing cards, or rolling dice;  know that samples from experiments will be different,  for example tossing a coin ten times, vary from one another, and from the proportions expected from a model, that is, most times five heads do not come up.

 

 

 

 

31L4

Probability

Know that probability is about the chance of outcomes occurring, and that it is not possible to know the exact probability of something occurring in most everyday situations; know that trialling must be used to gain information about the situation and that the results of trial samples vary; use methods such as listing, tree or network diagrams with equally likely outcomes, and tables to find all the possible outcomes of simple one or two stage situations such as tossing two coins, drawing counters from a bag, or rolling two dice; compare the distributions from trialling with the expectations obtained from models, accepting variation between samples and that the results of one sample do not impact on the next (independence).

31L5

Probability

Know that elements of chance have an effect on the certainty of results from surveys or experiments and that statistics involves situations where the probabilities are not known;  identify the theoretical probabilities for situations involving chance by using proportions of possible outcomes;  carry out experiments to test the probability of events and compare their results with theoretical probabilities; know that some variation between experimental estimates of probability and theoretical probabilities is normal, for example, when rolling a die 10 times they will not usually roll an even number 5 of the times; know that a larger sample is likely to provide a more accurate theoretical probability, proportionally speaking, than a small one; know that the results of past trials in probability experiments do not impact on future events.

32L4

Describing probability

Know that outcomes that are certain are described by fractions equalling one, including 100%, and outcomes that are impossible are described by fractions equalling zero, including 0%; know that the count of all possible outcomes gives the denominator of a probability fraction; accept variation from an exact fraction, for example 37 out of 100 were safe which is about or 33.3%; use simple fractions and percentages  like one half (50%), thirds (33.3% and 66.6%), quarters (25% and 75%), fifths (20%, 40%, 60%, 80%), tenths (10%, 30%, etc)

32L5

Calculating probability

Calculate probabilities for probability situations that involve two or more events, which may be independent (for example rolling two dice, or tossing two coins) or dependent (for example drawing two cards from a deck of cards without replacement, or choosing to students from the class at random);  model these situations using models such as tree diagrams, tables and systematic lists and assign theoretical probabilities as proportions using fractions, percentages and ratios, for example the odds of winning the game are 2:3; estimate probabilities given results of sufficient trials; know that such estimates are always approximate, and require use of equivalent proportions, for example an experiment results in a ratio of 43 blue: 79 red. An estimate of the probability of red is 2/3 or 67%.
